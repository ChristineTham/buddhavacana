Although [dependent origination](/buddhavacana/sutta/deporig) is compatible with the notion of "rebirth", and implies that our present (mental) condition can be traced back to past mental constructs, the [Khandhaka](https://tidipa.github.io/Khandhaka/) does not reference either `kamma` or the notion of rebirth.

Therefore, I suggest these concepts were not originally taught by the Buddha, but may be later clarifications (by himself or his disciples).

Many Buddhists believe the Buddha "invented" the concepts of `kamma` and rebirth but as Bronkhorst in **Greater Magadha**[^GreaterMagadha] points out, both notions were prevalent in other beliefs during the Buddha's time, and form part of the "fundamental spiritual ideology" of the Greater Magadha region. However, the notion of `kamma` in these beliefs was different from how the Buddha's later teachings defined it.

[^GreaterMagadha]: Bronkhorst, J. (2007). Greater Magadha. Brill.

`kamma` was regarded as the accumulation of spiritual "baggage" that "burden" or "encumber" the "soul", and cause rebirth. The metaphor is that `kamma` acts as a "weight" that binds the soul to another birth upon death (rather than presumably allowing the soul to ascend into heaven and even ultimately becoming completely free). The greater the "karmic burden", the lower the level of existence of the rebirth. Please note, this was not necessarily the Buddha's belief (he was probably ambivalent about whether this notion is true or not), but represents prevalent belief during his time.

It is also important to differentiate between the "traditional" notion of `kamma` vs how the Buddha has allegedly[^1] redefined it. The traditional view is that *all* actions leads to the accumulation of `kamma`, including both morally "good" actions and morally "bad" actions. The idea is that all actions have an element of "impurity", even supposedly innocuous actions such as sitting still breathing quietly (apparently even then it was recognised mere breathing can harm microscopic, living creatures).

[^1]: as we shall see later, it is doubtful whether he has in fact redefined it as the characterisation of `kamma` as "intention" is quoted somewhat out of context from a late sutta.

This led to the following different set of beliefs based on the above premise:

- Early Jainism: All actions lead to the accumulation of `kamma`, so to achieve liberation *all* activity must be suppressed. This led to the development of extreme ascetic practices â€” in which practitioners would remain motionless for very long stretches of time, and self-mortification was interpreted as bringing about the destruction of the traces of earlier deeds that had not yet suffered retribution. This is described in the earliest books of the ÅšvetÄmbara Jaina canon. The practitioner practices absorptive meditation (`sukkajjhÄá¹‡a`), abstains from food and eventually even breathing, and obtains liberation (from the cycle of rebirths) hopefully moments before death. The Buddha started his soteriological journey by practising Jain meditation techniques such as the dimension of nothingness and the dimension of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, which are techniques designed to suppress all thought and therefore avoid the generation of `kamma`. He then went on to practice extreme ascetism such as stopping his breath and stopping all intake of food. These are also techniques designed to stop the accumulation of `kamma`. The Buddha eventually abandoned these practices and presumably the Jain notion of `kamma`.
- Brahmanism: The Jain perspective was also adopted in the ÅšvetÄÅ›vatara Upaniá¹£ad and mentioned in the BhagavadgÄ«tÄ (according to [^GreaterMagadha]). According to Gombrich in **How Buddhism Began**[^Gombrich], the Upaniá¹£adic view of `karman` can be summarised as:

- Ä€jÄ«vikism: According to Bronkhorst, they also believed in the extreme ascetic practice of "voluntary death by starvation" but crucially did not believe this would hasten liberation, because they believed in a deterministic universe governed by the principle of order (`niyati`). There is no cause or basis for the purity of living beings, and all living beings must "serve their time" across 8,400,000 great `kappa`s (each `kappa` represents an aeon, or cycle of the evolution and dissolution of the universe). This did not necessarily preclude a belief in `kamma`, and perhaps the Ä€jÄ«vikas regarded it as a principle similar to entropy, so there is still a benefit in reducing `kamma` to a minimum in a deterministic universe, hence the ascetic practices.

As we can see, dependent origination is a radically original idea and necessitates a reconsideration of `kamma`. `kamma` is redefined (either by the Buddha but more likely a later disciple, presumably after the events narrated in the Khandhaka) as not necessarily caused by action, but by mental constructs, specifically "intention". Therefore `kamma` is an accumulation of previous mental constructs resulting in current mental condition.

However, note that the "Buddhist" concept of `kamma` is not present in dependent origination, nor in any of the earliest discourses. According to dependent origination, liberation is not dependent on accumulated `kamma`, it is achieved by the cessation of non-optimal mental constructions. There is nothing in the Buddha's conception of dependent origination that suggests existing or accumulated `kamma` will help or hinder the cessation and subsequent liberation. This is why I suggested the Buddha probably did not originally teach the concept of `kamma`, because it was irrelevant to the path to liberation, but it is useful as an "explanation" for current mental state.

Unfortunately, as Bronkhorst in **Did the Buddha Believe in Karma and Rebirth?**[^Bronkhorst1998] noted, the "[traditional] concept of karma keep on popping up within the Buddhist tradition. Interestingly, such practices and ideas are often presented as correct at one place in the early texts, and criticized and rejected at another.", Indeed, "... practices and ideas corresponding to the non-Buddhist concept of karma continued to exert an attraction on the Buddhists, even long after the days of 'early Buddhism'. I there drew attention to the practice of physical inactivity advocated by the Chinese master called MahÄyÄna in the 8th century in Tibet, and to the tathÄgatagarbha doctrine in MahÄyÄna Buddhism, which is so close to the non-Buddist idea of an inactive self that even some Buddhist texts draw attention to it." Often, modern Buddhists sometimes describe `kamma` more from the perspective of the "traditional" notion rather than the redefined "Buddhist" concept.

[^Bronkhorst1998]: Bronkhorst, J. (1998). Did the Buddha Believe in Karma and Rebirth? Journal of the International Association of Buddhist Studies, 21(1), 1â€“19.

An example of the traditional view of `kamma` making its way back into Buddhism is [13S4/1.3.5.1 Kammanirodhasutta](https://tipitaka2500.github.io/tipitaka/13S4/1/1.3/1.3.5/1.3.5.1.html), which advocated the cessation of all `kamma` (`kammanirodha`) - this is clearly an insertion of traditional Jain thinking.

Many scholars, such as Gombrich in several of his books, have declared that `kamma` is central to the Buddha's teachings and that he has redefined `kamma` to mean "intention."

But is this the case? Let's look at the actual sutta referenced by Gombrich - [16A6/2.1.9 Nibbedhikasutta](https://tipitaka2500.github.io/tipitaka/16A6/2/2.1/2.1.9.html) - I will quote the relevant section in full so that the full context can be appreciated:

The title of the sutta itself (`Nibbedhikasutta`) is an indication this is meant as a detailed exposition (`nibbedhikapariyÄyaá¹`) which implies it is probably an explanation crafted by a disciple, probably hundreds of years after the death of the Buddha. The text of the sutta clearly shows that `kamma` is clumsily inserted as part of the dependent origination chain. Moreover the use of `kamma` here is the ordinary usage of the word (to mean "actions" or "deeds"), and not some universal law of justice acting in accordance to merit or defilements - which is how a lot of Buddhists and even non-Buddhists refer to as "karma".

The sutta advocates the "cessation of `kamma`" as part of the chain of cessations that leads to liberation. This insertion into the chain is clumsy because liberation via the eightfold path requires "action" (`kamma`) - in fact, it requires "right action" (`sammÄkammanta`) which this sutta itself points out.
